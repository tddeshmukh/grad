This week, I focused on making improvements to the photoacoustic effect model. I also did some background reading on heat conduction and thermoelastic wave propagation so that I can work towards mathematically explaining the physics behind the model in future weeks.

Photoacoustic Effect Modeling

Two of the main components that were added to last week’s model are the implementation of pulsed laser light rather than a steady beam of light, and boundaries that enabled the heat to flow out of the silicon diaphragm. The latter of these two involved adding another component to the model- the “backplate” of MEMs microphones.

Pulsed Light

The pulsed light implemented in the model has a duty cycle of 20% and a period of 5ms. The temperature curve and magnitude of vibrations corresponding to the pulsed laser is shown below:

Because the temperature rises sharply at the beginning of the curve rather than experiencing a gradual increase, the pulsed laser does have an impact on heat conduction in the silicon. However, the temperature increase for following pulses is not as abrupt, and this translates to reduced vibrations through the silicon diaphragm.

Some factors that allow for greater increases in temperatures and larger vibrations include increasing the power of the laser and decreasing the thickness of the silicon diaphragm. Backplate

To allow heat waves to more accurately propagate through MEMs device components rather than being confined to the silicon diaphragm, we added a backplate. The backplate was fixed against thermal expansion, so the only vibration that occurs is in the silicon diaphragm:

The addition of this material changes both the temperature distribution and vibrations measured for the silicon diaphragm (excluding thermal changes of the backplate):

An interesting result is the sharp spike in temperature that is present in the first 10ms. This is related to how heat moves through conduction, the process by which heat is transferred through a solid by subsequent vibrations of neighboring molecules. The equation that describes heat transfer through a solid is known as Fourier’s law, and it is important to look into this mathematical description for our model so that we can better understand the foundations of thermoelastic wave propagation in the diaphragm.

Thermoelastic waves are what we are truly interested in observing since they describe the stresses on the material as a function of temperature. These stresses induce the vibrations which in turn can produce acoustic waves.

Next Steps

The next steps for improvement in the model is to add air to the surroundings of the diaphragm and backplate. Since air also has the ability to convert light to sound, this will involve not only adding heat transfer to the air, but also pressure acoustics to enable any resulting acoustic waves to affect the diaphragm. What we hope to see from this is a perceptible increase in magnitude for the vibrations on the diaphragm.

Conclusions from Early-Stage Modeling

Despite improvements on the model, the magnitude of vibrations (1e-10 meters) is still much lower than the thickness (1e-5 meters) of the diaphragm. The largest vibrations occur when temperature increases are rapid. However, the temperature plateaus that can be seen in each temperature graph indicate that the silicon is reaching thermal equilibrium with its surroundings. To get larger vibrations, heat must dissipate from the silicon faster into another medium, or silicon should start at a lower temperature.
